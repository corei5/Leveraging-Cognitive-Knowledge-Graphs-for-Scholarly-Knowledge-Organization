Leveraging Cognitive Knowledge Graphs for Scholarly Knowledge Organization

TIB—Leibniz Information Centre for Science and Technology, Hannover, Germany1
L3S Research Center, Leibniz University Hannover, Hannover, Germany 2

Abstract

The increasing number of published scholarly articles, exceeding 2.5 million yearly, presents challenges for researchers in keeping pace with scientific developments. Integrating scholarly article contributions into cognitive knowledge graphs (CKGs) enables structured access and organization of scholarly knowledge beyond titles and abstracts. This research focuses on utilizing large language models (LLMs) to categorize scholarly articles and describe their contributions in a structured and comparable manner. While prior efforts explored domain-specific applications of LLMs, their domain-independent knowledge and adaptability through prompt engineering or fine-tuning present opportunities for efficient, cost-effective, and sustainable scholarly knowledge modeling. Our methodology leverages LLM capabilities in combination with expert-verified data from a CKG to support scholarly tasks such as research field prediction, achieving up to 80% mean average score and 78% mean expert score with prefix prompting, and predicate recommendation, achieving up to 65% mean average score and 60% mean expert score with prefix prompting. We inject structured, domain-specific context into LLMs via graphlets derived from CKGs, employing both fine-tuning and a novel prompting technique. Our evaluation, based on AI-generated scores and expert judgments, demonstrates that CKG-informed prompting consistently improves performance across LLMs, while fine-tuning yields mixed results depending on the task and LLM size. Notably, expert evaluations reveal that LLMs sometimes make valid predictions not captured by incomplete gold standards, underscoring the importance of multi-perspective assessment. Our approach is integrated into the Open Research Knowledge Graph (ORKG), which provides precise access to organized scholarly knowledge and promotes domain-independent knowledge dissemination among researchers, policymakers, and the public.
